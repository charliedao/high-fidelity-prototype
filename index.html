FoundationWhy Do We Do Physical Assessments?

Physical assessment is the process by which the nurse reviews the body of a client for signs of disease or disorders. It generally follows the health history and is the FIRST step of the nursing process.

60-Second Assessment: Start every client encounter with a quick overall look — determine if problems are acute/chronic, life-threatening, and understand what is normal before identifying abnormal. Involve your patient in planning care.

Core Purposes

  • Reviews body for signs of disease or disorder
  • Follows the health history intake
  • Determines the client's individual needs
  • FIRST step of the nursing process
  • Identifies problems and care needs
  • Establishes a baseline for future comparison
  • Helps make clinical judgments

Nursing Process (ADPIE)

  • Assessment — collect data ← YOU ARE HERE
  • Diagnosis — identify problems
  • Planning — set goals
  • Implementation — carry out care
  • Evaluation — did it work?

Must KnowTypes of Data & Sources

Subjective Data

What the patient tells you — symptoms, feelings. Only the client can provide this.

  • Pain, nausea, dizziness, anxiety
  • "I feel tired" / "My chest hurts"
  • Document in quotes
  • Also called: Symptoms

Objective Data

What you observe, measure, or test — verifiable by another person.

  • BP 190/100, temp 38.5°C
  • Client appears pale, diaphoretic
  • Lab results, vital signs, exam findings
  • Also called: Signs
★ Trick: Subjective = patient Says it. Objective = you Observe it.
SourceTypeExamples
Patient themselvesPRIMARY ★ BestDirect interview, patient reports
Family membersSecondaryHistory when patient can't communicate
Other health professionalsSecondaryNurses, physicians, therapists
Diagnostic reportsSecondaryLab results, imaging, EKG
Textbooks / literatureSecondaryReference material

CommunicationData Collection & Interviewing
★ Two primary methods of data collection: Interview and Physical Examination

Interview Types

  • Structured (Directive) — nurse-driven; follows a set format; efficient
  • Non-structured (Non-directive) — client-controlled; rapport building; open-ended

3 Stages of an Interview

  • Opening: Establish rapport, explain purpose
  • Working: Collect health history and data
  • Closing: Summarize, confirm, thank client

Critical ContentVital Signs

The 6 Vital Signs

  • Temperature (T)
  • Pulse / Heart Rate (P)
  • Respirations (R)
  • Blood Pressure (BP) — Systolic & Diastolic
  • Oxygen Saturation (SpO₂)
  • Pain — the 6th vital sign

Pulse Abnormalities

⚡ Tachycardia (>100 bpm)

  • Fainting / lightheadedness
  • Rapid palpitations
  • Fluttering in chest
  • Bounding pulse
  • Chest pressure, SOB

🐌 Bradycardia (<60 bpm)

  • Fatigue or weakness
  • Dizziness, confusion
  • Fainting (syncope)
  • SOB
  • Difficulty exercising
Pulse Strength Scale: 0=absent · 1+=diminished · 2+=normal (brisk) · 3+=strong · 4+=bounding

Respiration Patterns

  • Eupnea: 12–20/min (normal adult)
  • Tachypnea: >20/min — SOB, cyanosis, retractions
  • Bradypnea: <10/min — dizziness, weakness, confusion
  • Apnea: No breathing — 🚨 EMERGENCY
  • Kussmaul: Deep, rapid, labored — DKA
  • Cheyne-Stokes: Alternating fast/slow + apnea — brain injury, heart failure
  • Orthopnea: Difficulty breathing flat — CHF, COPD
  • Dyspnea: Subjective SOB — patient reports difficulty

SpO₂ (Oxygen Saturation)

LevelStatusAction
≥95%NormalContinue monitoring
91–94%Mild hypoxemiaNotify provider; O₂ supplement
86–90%ModerateUrgent intervention needed
<85%🚨 SevereEmergency — act NOW

Pain Scales

  • NRS (Numeric): Adults — rate 0–10; 0=none, 10=worst imaginable
  • Wong-Baker FACES: Children ages 3–7 and communication barriers
  • FLACC: Infants & non-verbal — Face, Legs, Activity, Cry, Consolability
  • PAINAD: Dementia patients

Part II — First LookGeneral Survey

What the General Survey Includes

  • Physical appearance — body build, height, weight vs. age/lifestyle
  • Body structure — proportionate vs. excessively thin/obese
  • Overall hygiene & grooming — clean/neat vs. disheveled/dirty
  • Signs of distress — posture, facial expression, pain behaviors
  • Obvious signs of illness — skin color, breathing effort, pallor
  • Mobility — gait, posture, coordination
  • Behavior — attitude, affect, appropriateness
  • Vital signs — included in every general survey
Assessment DataNormal FindingsAbnormal Findings
Body build, height, weightProportionate; varies with lifestyleExcessively thin or obese
Hygiene and groomingClean, neatDisheveled, dirty, unkempt
Distress (posture/expression)No apparent distressWincing, grimacing, labored breathing, bent over in pain
Signs of health/illnessWell-developed, well-nourished, intact skin, easy breathingPallor, weakness, lesions, cough
Speech (quantity & quality)Understandable, moderate pace, clear toneRapid/slow pace, overly loud or soft
★ Cultural Considerations: Always consider cultural norms when interpreting hygiene, dress, communication style, and pain expression. What is "normal" varies widely across cultures.

NeurologicalMental Status & Neurological Assessment

Mental Status Components

  • General appearance
  • State of consciousness (LOC)
  • Mood and affect — appropriate to situation?
  • Thought content — logical, relevant, realistic?
  • Intellectual capacity
  • Appropriateness of responses

LOC Levels (Alert → Comatose)

LevelResponse
AlertFully awake; responds appropriately to all stimuli
LethargicDrowsy; arousable; returns to sleep quickly
ObtundedDifficult to arouse; responds to vigorous stimulation only
StuporousResponds only to painful stimuli; minimal verbal response
ComatoseUnconscious; no response to stimuli
Assessment DataNormal FindingsAbnormal Findings
Attitude (frame of mind)Cooperative, able to follow instructionsNegative, hostile, withdrawn, anxious
Affect / moodAppropriate to the situationInappropriate; sudden mood swings; paranoia
Thought organizationLogical sequence; relevant answers; sense of realityIllogical sequence; flight of ideas; confusion; vague

PERRLA

Pupils Equal, Round, Reactive to Light and Accommodation

  • Normal pupil size: 2–5 mm; equal bilaterally; react briskly to light
  • Anisocoria = unequal pupils → possible brain injury, herniation — 🚨 REPORT IMMEDIATELY
  • Fixed & dilated = severe brain injury or death
  • Miosis (constricted pupils) = opioids, certain eye drops

Head-to-ToeHEENT — Head, Eyes, Ears, Nose, Throat

Head & Scalp

  • Inspect hair distribution and texture
  • Assess scalp for lesions, tenderness
  • Inspect and palpate skull for lumps/bumps
  • Check facial symmetry — ask client to smile, puff cheeks

Eyes & Vision

  • Pupil size and shape (PERRLA)
  • Pupillary light reflex
  • Visual acuity (Snellen chart)
  • Inspect conjunctiva, sclerae
  • Extraocular movements

Oral Assessment

  • Lips: moisture, color, lesions
  • Teeth: missing/dentures
  • Oral mucosa: moisture, color
  • Gingivae (gums): swelling, bleeding
  • Tongue: position, coating, movement
  • Breath odor: ammonia, acetone, foul = abnormal
★ Facial Symmetry: Ask client to smile and puff out cheeks. Asymmetry can indicate cranial nerve (CN VII — facial nerve) damage or stroke.

CardiovascularCardiovascular & Peripheral Vascular Assessment

Heart Sounds

  • S1 ("Lub"): Closure of mitral & tricuspid valves — beginning of systole
  • S2 ("Dub"): Closure of pulmonic & aortic valves — beginning of diastole
  • Normal: "Lub-Dub" — any extra sounds are abnormal
★ Heart Auscultation Sites (A-P-E-T-M):
Aortic — right sternal border, 2nd ICS
Pulmonic — left sternal border, 2nd ICS
Erb's Point — left sternal border, 3rd ICS
Tricuspid — left sternal border, 4th ICS
Mitral — left midclavicular line, 5th ICS (Apical)

Apical Heart Rate

  • Location: midclavicular line, 5th intercostal space (adults)
  • Use stethoscope
  • Listen for one full minute
  • Each "lub-dub" = ONE beat

Capillary Refill Time (CRT)

  • Blanch nail bed with pressure
  • Normal: <3 seconds
  • Prolonged ≥3 seconds = poor perfusion, dehydration

Arterial Pulse Locations

PulseLocationSpecial Note
CarotidNeck, medial to sternocleidomastoid⚠ NEVER palpate both sides simultaneously — can cause syncope!
BrachialAntecubital fossa (inner elbow)Used for BP auscultation
RadialBelow base of thumb; medial/ventral side of wristMost common for routine checks
FemoralGroin / inguinal areaStrongest peripheral pulse
PoplitealBack of kneeMay require deep palpation
Posterior TibialBehind and slightly below the medial malleolus of ankleCheck for peripheral vascular disease
Dorsalis PedisMedial dorsum of foot (top of foot)Can be difficult to palpate; may be congenitally absent

FeatureVenous InsufficiencyArterial Insufficiency

Edema — Causes & Grading

Common Causes:

  • Heart failure
  • Venous insufficiency
  • Right-sided heart failure
  • Varicosities
  • Thrombophlebitis

Pitting Edema Scale:

  • 1+ — 2mm indentation; disappears rapidly
  • 2+ — 4mm; disappears in 10–15 seconds
  • 3+ — 6mm; disappears in 1–2 minutes
  • 4+ — 8mm; indentation lasts >2 minutes
★ Doppler: Non-invasive ultrasound device used to estimate blood flow through blood vessels when pulse is difficult to palpate using high-frequency sound waves.

AbdomenAbdominal Assessment

★ Abdominal Assessment Order: I-A-P-P

  • INSPECTION — symmetry, shape/contour, umbilicus, movement, enlarged organs, masses
  • AUSCULTATION — use diaphragm; bowel sounds every 5–15 seconds; all 4 quadrants; BEFORE palpation!
  • PALPATION — light palpation; gentle dipping motion; systematic approach; identify tenderness
  • PERCUSSION — assess underlying tissue; organ size

GenitourinaryGenitourinary Assessment

Bladder Facts

  • Bladder capacity: 600–1000 mL
  • Urge to void felt at approximately 200 mL
  • Sensation to void every 2–4 hours
  • Patients should void approximately 30 mL/hour
  • Bladder not palpable unless full/distended
  • Bladder scanner: non-invasive ultrasound to measure post-void residual
TermDefinitionStatus / Action
Normal output30–60 mL/hr (~1 mL/kg/hr)Normal
NocturiaUrinating more than once per nightAbnormal if frequent; disrupts sleep

AnuriaNo urine output (<100 mL/day)🚨 EMERGENCY
Urinary RetentionInability to empty bladder; distended bladder palpableMay require catheterization

SkinIntegumentary Assessment

What to Assess

  • Skin's general appearance — color, moisture, turgor, temperature
  • Signs of dehydration — poor turgor, dry mucous membranes
  • Skin turgor: pinch skin on back of hand/forearm; normal = returns immediately
  • Pressure ulcers / injuries — use Braden Scale to evaluate risk
  • Bony prominences — heels, sacrum, elbows, occiput, lateral malleoli
  • Surgical wounds — assess drainage, approximation, signs of infection

★ Memory Tricks — The Best Ones

  • IAPP = Inspect → Auscultate → Palpate → Percuss (ABDOMEN ORDER — ALWAYS)
  • Subjective = Symptoms (patient SAYS it). Objective = Signs (you SEE/measure it)
  • PRIMARY source = Patient. SECONDARY = Everyone/everything else
  • ABduction = Away. ADduction = ADD back toward midline
  • SUPination = hold SOUP (palm UP). Pronation = pour it out (palm DOWN)
  • PERRLA = Pupils Equal, Round, Reactive to Light and Accommodation
  • Crackles = fluid (CHF, pneumonia). Wheezing = bronchospasm (asthma). Stridor = EMERGENCY
  • S1 "Lub" = mitral + tricuspid close. S2 "Dub" = pulmonic + aortic close
  • NEVER palpate both carotids at once — can cause syncope/cardiac arrest
  • Cyanosis = blue = low O₂. Jaundice = yellow = liver. Pallor = pale = anemia/shock
  • Oliguria = <30 mL/hr = CALL the doctor! Anuria = no urine = EMERGENCY!
  • Cuff too small = falsely HIGH BP. Cuff too large = falsely LOW BP
  • Never take BP: IV side · mastectomy side · cast/injury · dialysis access
  • LOC order: Alert → Lethargic → Obtunded → Stuporous → Comatose (progressively worse)
  • A-P-E-T-M = Aortic, Pulmonic, Erb's, Tricuspid, Mitral (heart auscultation sites)
