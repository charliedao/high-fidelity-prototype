FoundationWhy Do We Do Physical Assessments?

Physical assessment is the process by which the nurse reviews the body of a client for signs of disease or disorders. It generally follows the taking of a health history and is the FIRST step of the nursing process.

Core Purposes

  • Reviews body for signs of disease or disorder
  • Follows the health history intake
  • Determines the client's individual needs
  • Is the FIRST step of the nursing process
  • Identifies problems and care needs
  • Establishes a baseline for future comparison
  • Helps make clinical judgments

The Nursing Process

  • Assessment — collect data (THIS IS WHERE WE ARE)
  • Diagnosis — identify problems
  • Planning — set goals
  • Implementation — carry out care
  • Evaluation — did it work?
★ Remember: Assessment always comes first — you can't plan care without data!

3
Must KnowTypes of Data & Sources

Subjective Data

What the patient tells you — symptoms, feelings, perceptions. Only the client can provide this.

  • Pain, nausea, dizziness, anxiety
  • "I feel tired" / "My chest hurts"
  • Document in quotes whenever possible
  • Also called: Symptoms

Objective Data

What you observe, measure, or test — can be verified by another person.

  • BP 190/100, temp 38.5°C
  • Client appears pale, diaphoretic
  • Lab results, vital signs, exam findings
  • Also called: Signs
★ Memory Trick: Subjective = what the patient Says. Objective = what you Observe.

Sources of Data

SourceTypeExamples
Patient themselvesPRIMARY ★ BestDirect interview, patient-reported symptoms
Family membersSecondaryHistory when patient can't communicate
Other health professionalsSecondaryNurses, physicians, therapists
Diagnostic reportsSecondaryLab results, imaging, EKG
Textbooks / literatureSecondaryReference material for clinical decision-making
Q
During an admission assessment, the nurse collects objective and subjective data. What is an example of subjective data?
▾
  • a. The patient is asleep.
  • b. The patient is tearful.
  • c. The patient has facial grimacing.
  • d. The patient states, "I hurt all over."
✔ D — "I hurt all over" is what the patient reports — subjective. Options A, B, and C are all things the nurse observes — objective data.
Q
Which are acceptable secondary sources of data? (Select all that apply) — A. Patient   B. Family members   C. Other health professionals   D. Diagnostic reports   E. Textbooks
▾
✔ B, C, D, E — The patient is the PRIMARY source. All others (family, professionals, reports, books) are secondary sources.
Q
When documenting subjective data for the cardiovascular system, the nurse would include which of the following? — A. Vital Signs   B. Peripheral pulses   C. Chest pain   D. Heart sounds
▾
✔ C — Chest pain is what the patient reports. Vital signs, pulses, and heart sounds are all things the nurse measures/hears — objective data.

CommunicationData Collection & Interviewing
Q
What are the two PRIMARY methods used to collect data? — A. Written report by patient and family   B. Review of chart   C. Interview and physical examination   D. Review of physician's orders
▾
✔ C — Interview and physical examination are the two primary methods of data collection.

Interview Types

  • Structured (Directive) — nurse-driven; follows a set format; efficient
  • Non-structured (Non-directive) — client-controlled; rapport building; open-ended

3 Stages of an Interview

  • Stage 1 — Opening: Establish rapport, explain purpose
  • Stage 2 — Working: Collect health history and data
  • Stage 3 — Closing: Summarize, confirm accuracy, thank client

Question Types

TypeExampleBest ForWatch Out
Open-ended"Tell me about your pain."Building rapport, broad info gatheringTakes more time
Closed"Is your pain sharp?"Quick facts, efficient chartingMay miss emotional context
Leading ⚠ AVOID"You're not in pain, are you?"Never — biases the responseSkews patient answers
Neutral ✓ Best"How would you describe your pain?"Professional, unbiasedPreferred in clinical practice

Open-Ended vs. Closed — Comparison

✅ Open-Ended Advantages

  • Builds trust and rapport
  • Client does the talking
  • Reveals what patient finds important
  • May reveal lack of patient knowledge
  • Can uncover info nurse wouldn't think to ask

❌ Disadvantages

  • Takes more time
  • Difficult to document
  • Language barriers possible
  • Nurse must control the interview

✅ Closed Advantages

  • More controlled / efficient
  • Requires less effort from client
  • Takes less time
  • Easily documented
  • Can get info sooner

❌ Disadvantages

  • May miss emotional information
  • Nurse may dominate interview
  • Doesn't allow volunteered info
  • May inhibit communication

Pulse — Assessment & Abnormalities

Use pads of index and middle fingers. Palpate both sides and compare for symmetry. All pulses should be regular in rhythm and equal in strength.

Radial Pulse: Below the base of the thumb — medial and ventral side of the wrist. Most common site for routine checks.

✓ Normal (60–100 bpm)

  • Regular rhythm
  • Equal bilateral strength
  • Strong and bounding

⚡ Tachycardia (>100 bpm)

  • Fainting / lightheadedness
  • Rapid palpitations
  • Fluttering in chest
  • Bounding pulse
  • Chest pressure, SOB
  • Fatigue

🐌 Bradycardia (<60 bpm)

  • Fatigue or weakness
  • Dizziness
  • Confusion
  • Fainting (syncope)
  • SOB
  • Difficulty exercising

★ Cuff size matters! Too small = falsely HIGH reading. Too large = falsely LOW reading. Always use the correct size!

⛔ NEVER take BP on:

  • Arm with injury or cast
  • Arm with IV site
  • Mastectomy side
  • Arm with dialysis access

✅ BP can be taken on:

  • Upper arm (most common)
  • Lower arm
  • Thigh
  • Lower extremity

🔺 Hypertension Signs

  • Severe headache
  • SOB / difficulty breathing
  • Nosebleeds (epistaxis)
  • Vision problems
  • Fatigue or confusion
  • Chest pain
  • Irregular heartbeat

🔻 Hypotension Signs

  • Dizziness / lightheadedness
  • Nausea
  • Fainting (syncope)
  • Lack of concentration
  • Blurred vision
  • Cold, clammy, cool skin
★ Orthostatic Hypotension: Measure BP lying → sitting → standing. A drop of ≥20 mmHg systolic OR ≥10 mmHg diastolic when changing positions = orthostatic hypotension. Patient may feel dizzy or faint when standing. Also called postural hypotension. Common in elderly, dehydrated, or patients on antihypertensives.
★ Which arm? Check: limb restriction band · sign over the bed · ask client · jewelry/IVs · whiteboard notation.

Pain — The 6th Vital Sign

  • Numeric Rating Scale (NRS): Adults — rate pain 0–10; 0 = no pain, 10 = worst imaginable
  • Wong-Baker FACES: Children ages 3–7; also used for adults with communication barriers
  • FLACC Scale: Infants & non-verbal patients; Face, Legs, Activity, Cry, Consolability
  • PAINAD: Dementia patients — breathing, vocalization, facial expression, body language, consolability

  • Review PMH, medications (OTC/herbal), immunization records

  • Social history: support systems, living situation, ADLs
  • Barriers: hearing, vision, cognitive, language
  • Active lifestyle → self-worth, relationships, intellectual function
  • Unresolved crisis → despair, hopelessness, bitterness
  • Evaluate environment; allow extra time; involve family

PERRLA

Pupils Equal, Round, Reactive to Light and Accommodation

  • Normal: pupils equal in size (2–5mm), round, react briskly to light
  • Unequal pupils = anisocoria → possible brain injury, stroke, herniation — 🚨 report immediately
  • Fixed and dilated pupils = severe brain injury or death
  • Constricted pupils (miosis) = opioids, certain eye drops
  • Dilated pupils (mydriasis) = stimulants, fear, certain medications

Atelectasis

Collapse of lung tissue (alveoli). Very common postoperatively due to shallow breathing under anesthesia. Causes decreased breath sounds over the affected area, possible fever, and SOB.

★ Prevention: Deep breathing exercises, incentive spirometry, early ambulation, coughing and splinting the incision.

Vascular — Arterial vs. Venous Insufficiency

Venous Insufficiency

  • Inadequate blood return via veins
  • Edema in legs/ankles
  • Varicosities (varicose veins)
  • Brown/rust discoloration (hemosiderin)
  • Stasis ulcers near ankle/gaiter area
  • Skin may be warm

Arterial Insufficiency

  • Inadequate arterial blood flow
  • Pain with walking (claudication)
  • Pale, cool, hairless skin
  • Weak or absent pulses
  • Ulcers on toes or pressure points
  • Shiny, taut skin
★ Edema: Abnormal accumulation of fluid in interstitial spaces. Can be pitting (finger leaves an indentation) or non-pitting. Grade 1–4+ scale.

Exam PrepPractice Questions
★ Instructions: Click a question to expand it. Click the answer choices to test yourself, then reveal the full explanation.
1
The nurse is preparing to assess a client's abdomen. In which order should the nurse use assessment techniques?
▾
  • a. Palpation, percussion, inspection, auscultation
  • b. Inspection, auscultation, percussion, palpation
  • c. Auscultation, inspection, percussion, palpation
  • d. Inspection, percussion, palpation, auscultation
✔ B — Inspection → Auscultation → Percussion → Palpation
For the abdomen, auscultation comes BEFORE palpation and percussion because palpating or percussing first can stimulate or alter bowel sounds, giving you an inaccurate assessment.
2
A nurse measures a client's blood pressure as 158/96 mmHg. How should this be classified?
▾
  • a. Normal
  • b. Elevated
  • c. Hypertension Stage 1
  • d. Hypertension Stage 2
✔ D — Hypertension Stage 2
Stage 2 = systolic ≥140 OR diastolic ≥90. This patient has BOTH (158 systolic and 96 diastolic), firmly placing them in Stage 2 hypertension.
3
Which respiratory pattern is associated with diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)?
▾
  • a. Cheyne-Stokes
  • b. Kussmaul breathing
  • c. Bradypnea
  • d. Orthopnea
✔ B — Kussmaul Breathing
Kussmaul is deep, rapid, labored breathing — the body's attempt to blow off CO₂ to compensate for the metabolic acidosis in DKA. It's a compensatory mechanism, not a primary problem.
4
A nurse notes bluish discoloration around the client's mouth. How should this be documented?
▾
  • a. Jaundice
  • b. Pallor
  • c. Circumoral cyanosis
  • d. Erythema
✔ C — Circumoral Cyanosis
Circumoral = around the mouth. Cyanosis = bluish discoloration from low oxygen. This is a sign of hypoxia and should be assessed and reported immediately.
5
The nurse auscultates a high-pitched, musical, whistling sound on expiration. This is BEST described as:
▾
  • a. Crackles
  • b. Rhonchi
  • c. Stridor
  • d. Wheezing
✔ D — Wheezing
Wheezing is a high-pitched musical whistling sound, typically on expiration, caused by bronchospasm (narrowed airways). Classic in asthma and COPD. Stridor is similar but is inspiratory and is a medical emergency from upper airway obstruction.
6
Which finding indicates the nurse should assess for orthostatic hypotension?
▾
  • a. Client reports dizziness when standing
  • b. Client has a BP of 120/80
  • c. Client has a resting heart rate of 72
  • d. Client reports difficulty sleeping
✔ A — Dizziness when standing
This is the classic symptom of orthostatic hypotension. The nurse should then measure BP in all three positions (lying, sitting, standing). A drop of ≥20 mmHg systolic or ≥10 mmHg diastolic confirms orthostatic hypotension.
7
A client's urine output is 20 mL/hour. How should the nurse interpret this?
▾
  • a. Normal output
  • b. Diuresis
  • c. Oliguria
  • d. Polyuria
✔ C — Oliguria
Oliguria = urine output <30 mL/hr. 20 mL/hr is below this threshold and must be reported to the provider immediately. Normal urine output is 30–60 mL/hr (or ~1 mL/kg/hr).
8
The nurse asks a patient, "Tell me about what brought you to the hospital today." This is an example of:
▾
  • a. Closed question
  • b. Leading question
  • c. Open-ended question
  • d. Directive question
✔ C — Open-ended question
Open-ended questions invite the patient to elaborate and lead the conversation. They typically start with "tell me," "describe," "what," or "how." This type of question builds rapport and reveals what the patient thinks is important.
9
The nurse notes a client's pupils are unequal in size. What is the correct term for this finding?
▾
  • a. PERRLA
  • b. Anisocoria
  • c. Miosis
  • d. Mydriasis
✔ B — Anisocoria
Anisocoria = unequal pupil size. This can indicate a neurological emergency such as increasing intracranial pressure, stroke, or uncal herniation. Report immediately!
10
A client's SpO₂ is 88%. What is the nurse's PRIORITY action?
▾
  • a. Document the finding and continue monitoring
  • b. Reposition the client and reassess
  • c. Notify the provider and anticipate supplemental O₂
  • d. Offer the client a glass of water
✔ C — Notify provider and apply supplemental O₂
SpO₂ of 88% = moderate hypoxemia (<90%). This requires urgent intervention — notify the provider immediately and apply supplemental oxygen per protocol. Do NOT just document and monitor.
11
Which breath sound is considered a medical emergency?
▾
  • a. Crackles
  • b. Rhonchi
  • c. Stridor
  • d. Wheezing
✔ C — Stridor
Stridor is a harsh, high-pitched inspiratory sound indicating upper airway obstruction. It is audible WITHOUT a stethoscope and requires immediate intervention. Think: anaphylaxis, foreign body, croup, epiglottitis.
12
The nurse notes a client responds only to painful stimuli. This LOC level is documented as:
▾
  • a. Lethargic
  • b. Obtunded
  • c. Stuporous
  • d. Comatose
✔ C — Stuporous
Stuporous = responds only to painful stimuli; very minimal verbal or motor response. Remember the order: Alert → Lethargic → Obtunded → Stuporous → Comatose.
13
A postoperative patient has no bowel movement and the nurse hears very infrequent, low-pitched bowel sounds. This is:
▾
  • a. Hyperactive bowel sounds
  • b. Hypoactive bowel sounds
  • c. Normal bowel sounds
  • d. Borborygmi
✔ B — Hypoactive bowel sounds
Hypoactive bowel sounds are infrequent and quiet; very common after surgery (paralytic ileus) due to anesthesia effects on peristalsis. Document and report; encourage ambulation and assess for signs of obstruction.
14
Which position BEST promotes oxygenation for a patient in severe respiratory distress?
▾
  • a. Trendelenburg
  • b. Supine
  • c. Orthopneic (tripod)
  • d. Prone
✔ C — Orthopneic / Tripod Position
Sitting upright and leaning forward on arms maximizes chest expansion and allows the diaphragm to move freely downward. This is the position patients with severe COPD or CHF naturally assume when they can't breathe.
15
A patient has an IV in the left arm and had a right-side mastectomy. Where should the nurse take the blood pressure?
▾
  • a. Left arm above the IV
  • b. Right arm
  • c. Ask the provider which arm to use
  • d. Thigh or lower extremity
✔ D — Thigh or lower extremity
Never take BP on an arm with an IV (disrupts flow, inaccurate reading) OR on the side of a mastectomy (lymphedema risk). With both arms unavailable, use the thigh or lower extremity instead.

★ Memory Tricks — The Best Ones

  • IAPP = Inspect → Auscultate → Palpate → Percuss (abdomen exam order — always!)
  • Subjective = Symptoms (patient SAYS it). Objective = Signs (you SEE/measure it).
  • PRIMARY source = Patient. SECONDARY = Everyone and everything else.
  • ABduction = Away from midline. ADduction = ADD back toward midline.
  • SUPination = hold SOUP in your palm (palm UP). Pronation = pour it out (palm DOWN).
  • PERRLA = Pupils Equal, Round, Reactive to Light and Accommodation.
  • Crackles = fluid (CHF, pneumonia). Wheezing = bronchospasm (asthma). Stridor = EMERGENCY (obstruction).
  • Cyanosis = blue = low O₂. Jaundice = yellow = liver. Pallor = pale = anemia/shock.
  • Oliguria = <30 mL/hr = CALL the doctor! Anuria = no urine = EMERGENCY!
  • Cuff too small = falsely HIGH. Cuff too large = falsely LOW.
  • Never take BP: IV side, mastectomy side, cast/injury, dialysis access side.
  • Fever > 100.4°F (38°C). Normal oral = 98.6°F. Rectal = +1°F. Axillary = –1°F.
  • Dorsiflexion = toes UP (like a door opening upward). Plantar flexion = toes DOWN (ballet dancer).
  • Alert → Lethargic → Obtunded → Stuporous → Comatose (LOC getting progressively worse).
